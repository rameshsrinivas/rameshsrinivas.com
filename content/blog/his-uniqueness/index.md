I was on the way to Cornell with my family when Federer lost what we didn't know at the time, but now know, was the last singles match of his career.

A few weeks later, the retirement announcement came. Expected, but not really. Not the way you can prepare for something like this.

I won't say I was depressed. But I was not myself.

Some of it was the news. More of it, I think, was that my son wasn't around — he's away on the west coast — and there was no one in the room with whom to share the particular grief of this particular ending.

Twenty-four years of obsession ends, and you find that the thing you've been carrying isn't really tennis. It's the lives that got built around it.

## How it started

I've been in love with the sport ever since I watched the 1980 Wimbledon final between Borg and McEnroe. The fourth-set tiebreaker — neither of them able to string two points together for most of it — left me wanting more.

Growing up in cricket-crazy India, tennis programming was hard to find unless it was a Grand Slam. I would *steal* the TV from my dad to watch Borg, McEnroe, Connors, Lendl. Over time, I converted him into a fan too.

Stefan Edberg was my first real tennis idol. While I liked others of his era — Becker among them — I was too invested in Stefan. My emotions rode along with his results, to the point where my mom could tell what had happened in tennis that day just by looking at me.

Little did I know that my emotional investment in Edberg would pale in comparison to what was coming.

## 1998 US Open

"Watch out for these two," said Dick Enberg.

Two scrawny kids, the next generation of greats. One was Juan Carlos Ferrero. The other was a Swiss kid with a weird last name.

I sat up watching them play. There was something about the Swiss kid that got my heart racing. He was making a ton of errors. But he was making tennis look so easy, so effortless, with such purity of shot-making.

I knew then.

## The obsession years

Like a lot of Federer fans, I followed the meteoric rise. It wasn't really the results. It was the unique style — often described as a spiritual experience — that pulled in hardcore fans and brand-new ones alike.

I was way too invested.

The world stopped when Roger was playing. It didn't matter what time zone, I was up. My wife and friends knew they had to plan schedules, events, and vacations around his matches. Wimbledon happens around our wedding anniversary, and my wonderful wife — knowing how much Roger meant to me — accommodated the obsession.

My little kids understood that daddy had to be left alone when this Federer guy played.

My son, then very young, figured out that the best way to spend quality time with dad was to sit next to him and watch the tennis. Just talk about it. Neither of us knew then how much of a bigger fan he'd turn out to be.

## 2008 US Open

We never missed a chance to watch Federer play on TV, but we hadn't seen him live until 2008. I was still smarting from the heartbreaker at Wimbledon — the loss to that precocious kid from Mallorca.

IBM invited me to their business suite at Arthur Ashe. They were kind enough to let me bring my five-year-old. It turned out to be a thrilling quarterfinal day — Djokovic vs. Robredo, then Federer vs. Andreev. Both went the distance.

Being that close to the action, it was clear that TV did no justice to the speeds and spins these guys generated. My son couldn't be bothered with Djokovic — Federer wasn't on yet — even as the people in the suite doted over my little man.

I knew Federer was popular. Neither my five-year-old nor I were prepared for the roar his announcement brought. What stood out immediately was how *built for tennis* his physique seemed. The crowd ooohed and ahhhed at every winner. I had goosebumps when I realized he had the entire stadium eating out of his hand without even trying.

He was the conductor of an orchestra, and he was playing all the instruments.

If I was a big fan walking in, I was a bigger one walking out.

That match may have been what turned my five-year-old from "yeah, my dad loves Federer" into a fan in his own right. We were bonding over Federer.

## The annual pilgrimage

Every year after that, we went to the US Open. We waited by the practice courts for autographs. We got nearly everyone — but never the elusive big whale, Roger himself.

While my son discussed most things with his mom, he discussed Federer with me like we were bros. We'd be disappointed in defeats together. We'd exult in wins together. He modeled his own tennis after Roger's, to the point where people watching him play remarked on the resemblance. He'd spend hours studying YouTube clips of impossible shot-making.

I went from waking him up at 4 a.m. for the Australian Open to him waking me up at 4 a.m. for the Australian Open.

When Roger lost to one of the big four, we'd be devastated together. We'd comfort each other through it. We'd seek out the positives.

*What would Roger do?*

## 2017 Australian Open

The 2017 Australian Open was an especially insane ride. We'd wake up early every morning, hearts pumping, hoping he could beat back time. As he won through his draw, we could relax. Until the final.

The final aged me several years. I'm sure it did the same to every other Federer fan.

I looked at my son at 1-3 down in the fifth. We looked at each other glumly. We knew what the other was thinking — *we've been here before and it's going to end the same way*.

What unfolded next is etched in me forever.

We were so pumped we woke up the rest of the house, and I swear we may have woken the neighbors. Could he really pull off the comeback, a break down in the fifth set against his longtime nemesis?

When he won, the both of us screamed at the TV. The next moment we hugged and jumped up and down. We both cried tears of joy.

I'm getting emotional writing this.

## My daughter joins

My daughter watched all this — the devotion, the ritual, the screaming at the TV — and wanted to understand what made us tick. Before long she was a convert. We'd go to the pre-Open practice sessions and watch Roger having an absolute blast, making it look easy peasy. It was her turn to bond with dad over Federer.

That elusive whale — Roger's autograph, the one my son couldn't snag for years — was eventually claimed by my daughter, even as she was getting crushed by the crowd. She immediately turned the ball over to her brother. She knew how much more it meant to him.

Another family bond, strengthened by a man we had never met.

## Don't cry because it's over

Far from being unique, I'm certain this kind of bonding through the shared love of an athlete is common. Roger Federer also created an extended family of fans around the globe.

It's been a rough week for us.

In the end, I ask the question: *what would Roger do?*

The answer is simple. Like Dr. Seuss said: *Don't cry because it's over. Smile because it happened.*

I'll cry for a little bit when it's all over. But I will smile always — because Roger Federer and "His Uniqueness" happened.

There will never be another like him. The tennis gods broke the mold. The sport will go on, bigger than any one person. But it will never be the same.
