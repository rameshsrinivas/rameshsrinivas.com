# Riya Prabhu for Student Body President — Senior Year Campaign Plan

## The Situation

Three-way race: Riya, another girl, and a boy (the 10th-grade winner). Risk of vote-splitting between the two female candidates. But Riya has something neither opponent has: **a proven track record across multiple years** and a reputation for actually delivering.

## Core Strategy: "She Gets It Done"

The campaign message is simple: **Riya doesn't just promise — she delivers.** She's the only candidate who has served as president, fought for budget increases, organized events, and shown up for students year after year. This isn't about gender or popularity — it's about who has earned the job.

The vote-splitting concern is neutralized by:
1. Locking in committed voters early (not just "I like Riya" but "I'm voting Riya")
2. Expanding reach into every grade through grade-level captains
3. Making the case that experience + results = the smart vote

---

## Phase 1: Build the Campaign Committee (Weeks 1-2)

### Grade Captains (8 people — 1 boy + 1 girl per grade)

Pick people who are:
- Well-connected in their grade (not necessarily popular — connected)
- Willing to actually talk to people, not just wear a button
- From different friend groups / activities / sports to maximize reach

| Grade | Role | What They Do |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Seniors (12th) | Co-Captains | Riya's inner circle. Organize senior support, coordinate messaging |
| Juniors (11th) | Grade Captains | Rally the grade that saw Riya deliver this year |
| Sophomores (10th) | Grade Captains | Key swing grade — they may not know Riya well yet |
| Freshmen (9th) | Grade Captains | Newest students, most impressionable, often overlooked by campaigns |

### How to recruit them
- Riya should personally ask each one — face to face, not over text
- Frame it as: "I need you specifically because you know people I don't reach"
- Give them a clear role from day one — people commit when they feel needed

### Action Team (4-6 people, any grade)

These are the doers — they don't need to be popular, they need to be reliable:

| Role | What They Do |
|------|-------------|
| **Poster Lead** (1-2 people) | Designs and prints posters, manages placement around school |
| **Social Media Lead** (1 person) | Runs private campaign content — Instagram stories, group chats, TikToks |
| **Slogan/Creative Lead** (1 person) | Writes taglines, comes up with chants, sticker ideas |
| **Event Coordinator** (1 person) | Helps plan any campaign events or visibility moments |

## Phase 2: Messaging & Materials (Weeks 2-3)

### The Message (pick one core tagline, use it everywhere)

Options to consider:
- **"Proven. Not Promised."** — highlights track record vs. empty promises
- **"She showed up. She delivered. She's not done."**
- **"Results, not resumes."**
- **"Four years of showing up for you."**

The message should always circle back to: *I've done this before, I delivered, and I have a plan for senior year.*

### Posters
- Keep them clean and bold — name, tagline, one visual
- Don't clutter with policy lists (save that for the speech)
- Place them in high-traffic spots: cafeteria, gym entrance, main hallways, bathrooms (if allowed)
- Refresh them — don't let the same poster go stale. Swap designs midway through

### Social Media (Private)
- Instagram stories: short clips of Riya with students, behind-the-scenes of campaign prep
- Have friends repost — the algorithm rewards shares
- "Why I'm voting Riya" 10-second clips from students in different grades/activities
- A highlight reel of things accomplished during her terms (events, changes, wins)

### Swag Budget ($100) — The Subliminal Campaign

The goal isn't just name recognition — it's *ambient repetition*. By election day, every student should have seen "Riya" dozens of times without ever being asked to pay attention. On water bottles in class, on backpacks in the hallway, on matching shirts walking past at lunch. The name becomes the default — the first thing that surfaces when they're staring at a ballot.

**Color:** Pick one bold, consistent color for everything — stickers, shirts, posters. Something that pops in a school hallway (bright blue, red, etc.). When that color becomes "Riya's color," every sighting reinforces the brand subconsciously, even from across the cafeteria.

| Item | Qty | Cost | Purpose |
|------|-----|------|---------|
| **Stickers** (2" circles) | 200+ | ~$20-25 | Mass awareness — the workhorse |
| **Committee t-shirts** | 5-6 | ~$45-50 | Social proof — makes the campaign look like a movement |
| **Poster supplies** | — | ~$20-25 | Hallway visibility |
| **Candy/treats** (election day) | — | ~$10 | Last-touch goodwill — people remember who gave them something |

#### Stickers: One Design, One Message

**"Vote Riya"** — that's it. Don't split into variants.

Why not "Riya for President"? It's informational but passive — states a fact, doesn't ask for anything. Why not the tagline? It doesn't have her *name* front and center. From 5 feet away on a water bottle, you need to read "Riya" instantly.

"Vote Riya" is a direct call to action. A command. It's what you want rattling around in someone's head when they're staring at the ballot.

- Bold font, one or two colors, dead simple
- 200 identical stickers — repetition is the point. When 50 kids have the same sticker on their water bottles, it looks like a movement. Mixed designs dilute that.
- Water bottles, laptops, phone cases, notebooks — these become walking ads every period, every day
- Sources: StickerMule, Sticker Giant, or a local print shop

#### Committee T-Shirts: Front is the Brand, Back is the Proof

**Front** (clean, centered):
> **PRABHU FOR PRESIDENT**

Last-name branding sounds *official* — like a real political campaign. It signals seriousness and authority. And it complements the stickers perfectly: stickers say "Vote Riya" (personal, familiar), shirts say "Prabhu for President" (formal, commanding). Two registers, same candidate.

**Back** (readable as they walk past in the hallway):
> **Pick Experience**
> ★ Vote Riya for President ★

"Pick Experience" frames the entire election around the one dimension where Riya has no competition. Every time someone reads it, the subconscious question becomes "who has experience?" — and there's only one answer.

#### How It All Works Together

The sticker says **Vote Riya** — name recognition, call to action, constant ambient exposure. The shirt says **Team Riya / She delivered** — credibility, momentum, social proof. The poster in the hallway ties it together visually with the same color. Three touchpoints, three different contexts, one unified message — and none of it feels like "campaigning." It just feels like Riya is everywhere.

## Phase 3: Vote-Splitting Defense (Ongoing)

This is the real game. Here's how to handle it:

### Do NOT attack the other girl
- Never. It backfires and makes both female candidates look bad while the boy skates through.
- If anyone brings up "but won't the votes split?" — Riya's answer: *"I'm not running against her. I'm running on my record."*

### Make the electorate think about qualifications, not personality
- The more the conversation is about "who has actually done this job," the more Riya wins
- Grade captains should be subtly steering conversations: *"I like [other girl] too, but Riya's actually been president and got us [specific thing]"*
- Frame it as: voting for Riya isn't a risk — you know exactly what you're getting

### Lock in votes early
- The biggest danger is people being undecided on election day and splitting randomly
- Grade captains should be having direct conversations: *"Can Riya count on your vote?"*
- Track soft commitments — know where you stand before the speech

### Win the freshmen
- Freshmen are the swing vote. They don't know any candidate well.
- Riya should make a point to be visible to freshmen — eat lunch near them, say hi in hallways
- Freshman grade captains are critical — they're Riya's ambassadors to an entire grade that has no loyalty yet

## Phase 4: The Speech (Final Week)

Riya already knows how to write a killer speech. Based on her past winners, the formula is:

1. **Open with shared experience** — something every student in the room is feeling right now
2. **Pivot to track record** — "I didn't just talk about it, I did it" (name 2-3 specific things)
3. **Vision for senior year** — 2-3 concrete, exciting ideas (not vague "make it the best year")
4. **Close with identity** — remind them who she is and why she cares

### Senior Year Speech Angles
- "This is our last year. It has to count. And I'm the one who knows how to make it count."
- Reference specific wins from past terms — the ice cream truck, the budget negotiations, the events
- Senior-specific ideas: prom input, senior week activities, graduation traditions, legacy projects
- End strong: "You've trusted me before. I delivered. Let's finish this together."

---

## Phase 5: Election Day

- Grade captains remind every soft-commit to actually vote
- Riya should be visible, confident, and warm all day — not nervous or campaign-y
- No last-minute drama or desperation moves
- Trust the work

## The Bottom Line

Riya's advantage is that she's not a first-timer making promises — she's a proven leader with receipts. The campaign should make that impossible to ignore. Every poster, every conversation, every social media post should reinforce: **she showed up, she delivered, and she's ready to make senior year count.**

The vote-splitting risk goes away when Riya is clearly the strongest candidate, not just one of two girls running. Make the race about track record, and she wins.
