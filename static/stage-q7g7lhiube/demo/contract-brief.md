# Microsoft–LinkedIn Merger Agreement — GC Executive Brief

**Source:** Agreement and Plan of Merger, dated June 11, 2016 (Exhibit 2.1)

---

## 1. Deal Snapshot
- **Parties:** Microsoft Corporation (Parent, WA) / Liberty Merger Sub Inc. (DE) / LinkedIn Corporation (Target, DE). Cash merger, Merger Sub into LinkedIn; LinkedIn survives as wholly-owned sub.
- **Consideration:** $196.00/share cash (§1.1(ss)); ~$26.2B equity value. Governing law: **Delaware** (§9.9).
- **Outside Date:** Jan 11, 2017, auto-extendable by either party to **June 11, 2017** if only regulatory conditions remain unmet (§8.1(c)).

## 3. Non-Standard Provisions

- **NON-STANDARD — 15% Acquisition Transaction threshold (§1.1(c))**
  *Market standard:* 20% (sometimes 25%) for large-cap strategic tech deals. *This draft:* 15% across all three prongs (voting power, assets, merger). Note the tail-period "50% deeming rule" in §8.3(b)(i) partially offsets this for fee-trigger purposes, but the in-term no-shop/fiduciary-out plumbing still uses 15%.

- **Termination fee = ~2.77% of equity value ($725M; §8.3(b))**
  *Market standard:* 3.0%–3.5% for strategic tech deals of this size. *This draft:* Notably **below market** — reflects LinkedIn's negotiating leverage (Salesforce was reportedly bidding) and keeps the door slightly cracked for a topping bid. Flag to board: the fee is not a strong deterrent.

- **NON-STANDARD — 3 business-day match right / 2 days on re-notice (§5.3(d)(ii)(3))**
  *Market standard:* 4–5 business days initial, 3 on re-notice. *This draft:* Compressed. Favors LinkedIn Board's ability to flip quickly; MSFT's match window is tight. Operational readiness matters — MSFT must be able to respond to a Superior Proposal in 72 hours.

- **NON-STANDARD — No go-shop (§5.3)**
  *Market standard for strategic tech:* No go-shop is typical (go-shops are sponsor-deal territory). *This draft:* Consistent with strategic norm — **not actually non-standard**, but worth confirming to the board since financial press often asks. Straight no-shop with fiduciary out.

- **NON-STANDARD — No Reverse Termination Fee (§8.3)**
  *Market standard:* $1–2B RTF for antitrust failure in deals of this size, or at minimum a regulatory-failure RTF. *This draft:* **There is none.** LinkedIn has no cash downside if MSFT fails to clear antitrust. Instead, LinkedIn relies on **specific performance** (§9.8(b)) to force closing. This is buyer-favorable on cash but exposes MSFT to forced-closing litigation risk.

- **Bilateral, full-scope specific performance (§9.8(b))**
  *Market standard:* Often conditional/capped specific performance (e.g., only if debt financing is funded). *This draft:* Unconditional, no bond required, explicitly "integral part of the Merger." Pairs with the no-RTF structure — LinkedIn's remedy is the deal itself, not cash.

## 4. Termination & Exit

| Trigger | Section | Who | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mutual written agreement | §8.1(a) | Both | No fee |
| Permanent injunction / illegal / final non-appealable order | §8.1(b) | Either | No fee (unless non-efforts) |
| Outside Date (Jan 11, 2017, extendable to June 11, 2017) | §8.1(c) | Either | No fee directly; tail fee possible under §8.3(b)(i) |
| Stockholder vote fails | §8.1(d) | Either | **$725M Termination Fee** payable by LinkedIn (§8.3(b)(iii)) |
| LinkedIn material breach (40-day cure) | §8.1(e) | MSFT | No fee at termination; **$725M tail fee** if LinkedIn signs/closes an Acquisition Transaction (deemed at 50% threshold) within 12 months (§8.3(b)(i)) |
| Company Board Recommendation Change | §8.1(f) | MSFT | **$725M Termination Fee** within 2 business days (§8.3(b)(ii)) |
| MSFT/Merger Sub material breach (40-day cure) | §8.1(g) | LinkedIn | **No reverse termination fee.** LinkedIn's remedy is specific performance (§9.8(b)). |
| LinkedIn accepts Superior Proposal | §8.1(h) | LinkedIn | **$725M Termination Fee** concurrent with termination (§8.3(b)(iii)), plus compliance with §5.3 match process |

**Fee paid once only** (§8.3(c)). **Termination fee is sole monetary remedy** against LinkedIn and its related parties (§8.3(e)) — but specific performance survives for both sides.

## 5. Open Questions for Counterparty's Counsel

1. **Why 15%, not 20%?** Was this a reopener from a prior draft, or did LinkedIn's bankers insist? Understanding origin tells us how defensible it is if a topping bid emerges.
2. **Pre-signing process** — was there a market check? Who else was in the room? The compressed 3-day match period implies confidence the process was already run; confirm before the board meets.
3. **Salesforce standstill status** — any standstill being waived under §5.3(a) (which permits waiver for confidential proposals to the Board)? Identify who the realistic topping bidders are.
4. **Regulatory strategy** — given the absence of any RTF, what efforts standard applies to antitrust, and what divestiture commitments has MSFT agreed to (see §6.1/§6.2 hell-or-high-water language)?
5. **Interim operating covenants (§5.2)** — any carve-outs for LinkedIn's planned hires, product launches, or capex that could trip §8.1(e) inadvertently during a 12-month pendency?
6. **Employee/retention mechanics (§2.8)** — treatment of unvested RSUs and any change-in-control acceleration; material to post-close retention cost modeling.
7. **Stockholder litigation playbook** — standard appraisal rights under DGCL §262 apply; confirm no special disclosure obligations have been negotiated.
8. **Debt (Notes Indenture, §6.16)** — what's the refinancing/assumption plan for LinkedIn's outstanding convertibles, and does MSFT's guarantee path create any cross-default exposure pre-closing?
